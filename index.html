So What is it?

The Flint water crisis is a drinking water contamination crisis in Flint, Michigan that started in April 2014. After Flint changed its water source from treated Detroit Water and Sewerage Department water to the Flint River, its drinking water had a series of problems that culminated with lead contamination, creating a serious public health danger.

Water sampled in Flint contained lead levels high enough to meet the EPA's definition of "toxic waste."

May 2014

Residents begin to complain about the water’s color, taste and odor, and to report rashes and concerns about bacteria. Government says “Flint water is safe to drink."

August 2014

City officials issue boil-water advisories after coliform bacteria are detected in tap water.

October 2014

A General Motors plant in Flint stops using municipal water, saying it corrodes car parts. The city arranges for the company to tap into a different water line. The residents of Flint still have to drink the river water.

February 2015

104 parts per billion of lead are detected in drinking water at a Flint home. The E.P.A. requires action at 15 parts per billion. Some side affects are...

Developmental delay

Vomiting

Memory loss

Declines in mental functioning

Learning difficulties

Constipation

Mood disorders

Pain

Februray 2015

A consultant hired by the city of Flint to investigate the water quality says the water is safe to drink despite the presence of sediment and discoloration.

Full Report

Setember 2015

A group of doctors in Flint urges the city to stop using the Flint River for water after finding high levels of lead in the blood of children. State regulators insist the water is safe.
A month later...
Flint reconnects to Detroit’s water. Residents are advised not to use unfiltered tap water for drinking, cooking or bathing.

December 2015

Flint declares an emergency.
It takes another month for Governor Snyder to declare a state of emergency for Genesee County, which includes Flint.

To The City

January 2016

President Obama declares a state of emergency in the city and surrounding county, allowing the Federal Emergency Management Agency to provide up to $5 million in aid.

Obama says he “would be beside myself” if he were a parent in the city. The Michigan House approves $28 million requested by the governor to assist the city.

In Flint, between 6,000 and 12,000 children have been exposed to drinking water with high levels of lead and they may experience a range of serious health problems.The water change is also a possible cause of an outbreak of Legionnaires' disease in the county that has killed 10 people and affected another 77.

Want to do something about it?

The American Red Cross has volunteers distributing water testing kits, water filters and bottled water at several locations.

The Flint Water Fund is accepting donations to help purchase water filters and bottled water.

Flint Child Health & Development Fund will "support the delivery of critical public health, medical, and community-based services and interventions" to help children in the city who were exposed to lead in the drinking water, according to its website.
